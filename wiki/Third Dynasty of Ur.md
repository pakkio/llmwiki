# Third Dynasty of Ur

The **Third Dynasty of Ur** (also called **Ur III**, c. 2112–2004 BC) was a powerful Sumerian dynasty based in the city of [[Ur]] that controlled much of [[Mesopotamia]]. It is the best-documented period of Sumerian history and represented the last great native Sumerian empire before the rise of the [[Amorites]] and [[Babylon]].

## Foundation

After the decline of the [[Akkadian Empire]], southern Mesopotamia came under the control of the [[Gutians]]. [[Utu-hengal]], a king of [[Uruk]], drove the Gutians out and took control of the region. Upon his death, his governor of Ur, [[Ur-Nammu]], moved the kingship to [[Ur]], ruling between c. 2047 BC and 2030 BC.

## Rulers

According to the [[Sumerian King List]], five kings of the Third Dynasty of Ur ruled for 108 years:

1. **[[Ur-Nammu]]** (c. 2112–2094 BC) – Founder of the dynasty. Built temples including the [[Ziggurat of Ur]], improved agriculture through irrigation, and issued the [[Code of Ur-Nammu]], one of the oldest known law codes.
2. **[[Shulgi]]** (c. 2094–2046 BC) – The greatest king of the dynasty. Solidified hegemony and reformed the empire into a highly centralized bureaucratic state. Ruled for at least 42 years and deified himself halfway through his rule.
3. **Amar-Sin** (c. 2046–2037 BC)
4. **Shu-Sin** (c. 2037–2028 BC)
5. **Ibbi-Sin** (c. 2028–2004 BC) – Last king. The empire fell around 1940 BC to the [[Elamites]] in his 24th regnal year.

## Administration

The Ur III empire was a highly centralized bureaucratic state. It controlled a vast territory through a system of provincial governors, and extensive administrative records survive in the form of [[cuneiform tablets]].

## Culture and Literature

During the Ur III period, hymns praising rulers and heroes became a popular literary genre. Toward the end of the period, a series of poems lamenting the decline of Ur were produced, including the famous *Lament over the destruction of Sumer and Ur*, which details the capture of King [[Ibbi-Sin]] by the [[Elamites]].

## Legacy

At its height, Ur was the largest city in the world from c. 2030 to 1980 BC, with a population of approximately 65,000. The dynasty's fall marked the end of Sumerian political dominance in Mesopotamia.

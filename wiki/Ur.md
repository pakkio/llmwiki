# Ur

**Ur** (Sumerian: 𒋀𒀕𒆠 *Urim*; Arabic: أُور) was a major [[Sumer]]ian city-state in ancient [[Mesopotamia]], located at the site of modern Tell el-Muqayyar in Dhi Qar Governorate, [[Iraq]]. Although it was originally a coastal city near the mouth of the [[Euphrates]] on the Persian Gulf, the coastline has shifted and the site is now well inland, about 16 km southwest of the city of Nasiriyah.

## Overview

The city dates from the [[Ubaid period]] (c. 3800 BC), and is recorded in written history as a city-state from the 26th century BC. Its first recorded king was [[Mesannepada]]. The city's patron deity was the moon god [[Nanna]] (Sin in Akkadian), and the name of the city is derived from *UNUGKI*, literally "the abode (of Nanna)".

The site is marked by the partially restored ruins of the [[Ziggurat of Ur]], which contained the shrine of Nanna, excavated in the 1930s. The temple was built in the 21st century BC during the reign of [[Ur-Nammu]] and was reconstructed in the 6th century BC by [[Nabonidus]], the last king of Babylon.

## Society and Culture

Ur was a major Sumerian urban center. The discovery of the [[Royal Tombs of Ur]] confirmed its immense wealth, containing luxury items made of precious metals and semi-precious stones imported from long distances (Ancient Iran, Afghanistan, India, Asia Minor, the Levant, and the Persian Gulf). The excavation in 1928 revealed the [[Lyres of Ur]], instruments similar to the modern harp.

Ur was an important religious center, home to the Temple of [[Inanna]]. When conquered by [[Sargon of Akkad]], he appointed his daughter [[Enheduanna]] as high priestess of Nanna. Control over the priesthood of Nanna remained a symbolic marker of political control for five centuries.

Ur was also significant for the production of [[Sumerian literature]]. [[Enheduanna]] is considered the potential first identifiable author in world history, composing hymns to various Mesopotamian deities. During the [[Third Dynasty of Ur|Ur III period]], hymns praising rulers and heroes became popular literary genres, as well as lamentations over the decline of Ur.

## History

The site consists of a mound roughly 1200 by 800 metres with a height of about 20 metres above the plain. Occupation size ranged from about 15 hectares in the Jemdet Nasr period to 140 hectares in the [[Isin-Larsa period]].

### Prehistory and Ubaid Period

When Ur was founded, it was near the coastline of the Persian Gulf, with marshy surroundings. Archaeologists have discovered evidence of early occupation during the [[Ubaid period]] (c. 5500–3700 BC). A layer of sediment covering Ubaid levels was initially interpreted as evidence for the [[Great Flood]] of the [[Epic of Gilgamesh]] and the [[Book of Genesis]].

### Early Dynastic Period

The [[First Dynasty of Ur]] seems to have had great wealth and power, as shown by the lavish remains of the Royal Cemetery. [[Mesannepada]] is the first king mentioned in the [[Sumerian King List]]. Five more kings followed until the First Dynasty came to an end when kingship was usurped by the city of Awan.

### Akkadian Period

Ur came under the control of the Semitic-speaking [[Akkadian Empire]] (c. 2334–2154 BC) founded by [[Sargon the Great]]. Sargon installed his daughter [[Enheduanna]] as high priestess of Nanna. The Akkadian period ended in the 22nd century BC due to internal and external pressures, particularly from the [[Gutians]].

### Ur III Period

The [[Third Dynasty of Ur]] (c. 2112–2004 BC) was founded by [[Ur-Nammu]], who built temples including the Ziggurat of Ur and issued the [[Code of Ur-Nammu]], one of the oldest known law codes. [[Shulgi]], the greatest king of the dynasty, reformed the empire into a highly centralized bureaucratic state. Ur was reportedly the largest city in the world from c. 2030 to 1980 BC, with a population of approximately 65,000.

The empire fell around 1940 BC to the [[Elamites]] in the 24th regnal year of [[Ibbi-Sin]], an event commemorated by the *Lament for Ur*.

### Later Periods

During the Middle Bronze Age, Ur was conquered by [[Hammurabi]] of the [[Old Babylonian Empire]]. During the reign of [[Samsu-iluna]], Ur was abandoned. It later became part of the native [[Sealand Dynasty]] for several centuries. After the decline of the Sealand Dynasty, Ur came under the control of the [[Kassites]] and later the [[Middle Assyrian Empire]].

In the Iron Age, Ur fell to the [[Neo-Assyrian Empire]] and later to the [[Neo-Babylonian Empire]]. The last Babylonian king, [[Nabonidus]], improved the ziggurat. The city started to decline from around 530 BC after Babylonia fell to the [[Persian Achaemenid Empire]] and was no longer inhabited by the early 5th century BC.

## Identification with the Biblical Ur

Ur is possibly the city of **Ur of the Chaldees (Ur Kasdim)** mentioned in the [[Book of Genesis]] as the birthplace of the Jewish patriarch [[Abraham]]. The biblical Ur is mentioned four times in the Torah with the distinction "of the Kasdim". In March 2021, [[Pope Francis]] visited Ur during his journey through Iraq.

## Archaeology

The site was first identified as Ur by [[Henry Rawlinson]] after deciphering bricks brought to England by [[William Loftus]]. Early excavations were conducted by [[John George Taylor]] (1853–1854, 1858). Major excavations from 1922 to 1934 were funded by the [[British Museum]] and the [[University of Pennsylvania]], led by [[Charles Leonard Woolley]]. A total of about 1,850 burials were uncovered, including 16 "royal tombs".

Recent excavations began in 2015 under the direction of Elizabeth C. Stone and Paul Zimansky of the [[State University of New York]]. Since 2009, the [[Global Heritage Fund]] has been working to protect and preserve Ur. Since 2013, the Italian Ministry of Foreign Affairs has been cooperating with the Iraqi State Board of Antiquities and Heritage for conservation.

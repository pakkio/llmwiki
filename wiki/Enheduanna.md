# Enheduanna

**Enheduanna** (also spelled Enheduana) was a high priestess of the moon god [[Nanna]] (Sin) in the Sumerian city of [[Ur]] during the [[Akkadian Empire]] period. She is considered the potential first identifiable author in world history.

## Biography

Enheduanna was the daughter of [[Sargon of Akkad]] (Sargon the Great), the founder of the [[Akkadian Empire]]. When Sargon conquered [[Ur]], he appointed her as high priestess of [[Nanna]], an act that historians such as [[Marc van de Mieroop]] interpret as a symbolic move to legitimize his rule and exert political power over southern [[Babylonia]].

Control over the priesthood of Nanna remained a symbolic marker of political control over the region of Babylon for five centuries following Sargon's reign.

## Literary Works

Enheduanna is credited with composing hymns to various Mesopotamian deities. Her most famous work is *The Exaltation of Inanna*, a hymn to the goddess [[Inanna]]. She also composed the **Temple Hymns**, a collection of poems dedicated to the temples of various Sumerian deities.

One of the Temple Hymns is dedicated to [[Ga'eš]] and the Karzida temple of [[Nanna]]/Sin there, which reads in part:

> "Shrine, great sanctuary?, founded at a cattle-pen, 'Small' city, ... of Suen Karzida, your interior is a ... place, your foundation is holy and clean..."

## Scholarly Debate

Assyriologists are unsure whether Enheduanna composed the hymns herself or whether a scribe composed them on her behalf. Nevertheless, she remains a significant figure in the history of literature and women's history.

## Legacy

Enheduanna's works were copied and studied for centuries after her death, becoming part of the scribal curriculum in Mesopotamia. She is celebrated as one of the earliest known named authors in world literature.

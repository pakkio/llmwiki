# Ziggurat of Ur

The **Ziggurat of Ur** (also known as the **Great Ziggurat of Ur**) is a [[Neo-Sumerian]] [[ziggurat]] located at the site of the ancient city of [[Ur]] in present-day [[Dhi Qar Governorate]], [[Iraq]]. It stands as the best-preserved and most visible landmark at the site.

## Description

The ziggurat was a massive stepped platform structure built from mud-brick, with a shrine dedicated to the moon god [[Nanna]] (Sin in Akkadian) at its summit. The structure was part of a temple complex that served as the religious center of [[Ur]].

## Construction and History

The ziggurat was originally built in the 21st century BC during the reign of [[Ur-Nammu]], the founder of the [[Third Dynasty of Ur]], and his successor [[Shulgi]]. It was later reconstructed in the 6th century BC by [[Nabonidus]], the last king of [[Babylon]].

Stamped bricks found on the ziggurat detail later rebuilding work, including reconstruction of the temple of [[Ningal]] by the [[Kassite]] ruler [[Kurigalzu I]] in the 14th century BC. Additional structures were added during the [[Isin-Larsa period]] under the [[Elamite]] kings of [[Larsa]].

## Excavation

The ziggurat was first uncovered by [[John George Taylor]] in the 1850s. [[Charles Leonard Woolley]] conducted extensive excavations of the structure between 1922 and 1934 as part of the joint [[British Museum]] and [[University of Pennsylvania]] expeditions.

## Current Status

Though some areas cleared during excavations have since been covered by sand, the Great Ziggurat remains fully cleared and is the site's most prominent landmark. It has modern graffiti lightly carved into its bricks. Since 2009, the [[Global Heritage Fund]] has been working to protect and preserve the ziggurat and the wider site of [[Ur]].

## Religious Significance

The temple on the ziggurat was dedicated to [[Nanna]], the moon god and patron deity of [[Ur]]. The city's name itself is derived from *UNUGKI*, meaning "the abode (of Nanna)". The ziggurat was closely tied to the _Akiti_ festival of Nanna, which began at the temple in Ur and ended at the shrine in [[Ga'eš]] (proposed to be modern [[Tell Sakhariya]]).

# Royal Tombs of Ur

The **Royal Tombs of Ur** are a series of approximately 1,850 burials uncovered at the site of the ancient city of [[Ur]] in present-day Iraq. Among these, 16 tombs were designated as "royal tombs" (dating to the [[Early Dynastic IIIa period]], approximately the 25th or 24th century BC), containing many valuable artifacts including the famous [[Standard of Ur]].

## Discovery and Excavation

The tombs were excavated between 1922 and 1934 by an expedition funded by the [[British Museum]] and the [[University of Pennsylvania]], led by archaeologist [[Charles Leonard Woolley]]. One of Woolley's assistants on the site was [[Max Mallowan]].

Excavators initially dug two trenches in the middle of the desert. After finding evidence of burial grounds with golden jewelry and pottery, they called it the "gold trench". By the end of six seasons, they had excavated 1,850 burial sites.

## The Royal Burials

The tombs contained luxury items made of precious metals and semi-precious stones imported from [[Ancient Iran]], [[Afghanistan]], [[India]], [[Asia Minor]], the [[Levant]], and the [[Persian Gulf]].

### Queen Puabi's Tomb

One of the most significant finds was the unlooted tomb of a queen thought to be **Queen Puabi** (formerly transcribed as Shub-ad), known from a cylinder seal found in the tomb. Many other people had been buried with her in a form of [[human sacrifice]].

### The King's Grave (PG-789)

Underneath a chest in Puabi's tomb, a hole led to what was called the "King's Grave" (PG-789). It contained 63 attendants equipped with copper helmets and swords, thought to be the king's army buried with him.

### The Great Death Pit (PG-1237)

Another large room, PG-1237, called the "Great Death Pit", contained 74 bodies, 68 of which were women. Two large ram statues were found here, believed to be the remains of lyres.

## Cause of Death

Woolley initially believed the attendants went to their deaths willingly by poison. However, computerized tomography scans on surviving skulls have shown signs of blunt force injuries, suggesting they were killed by blows to the head, possibly from the spiked end of a copper axe.

## Major Artifacts

- **[[Standard of Ur]]** – A mosaic of red limestone, bitumen, lapis lazuli, and shell, showing scenes of war and peace
- **[[Lyres of Ur]]** – Instruments similar to the modern harp but in the shape of a bull with eleven strings
- **Gold helmet of Meskalamdug** – A gold helmet of a king of Ur I
- **Ram in a Thicket** – A figurine found in the tombs
- **Royal Game of Ur** – A ancient board game

## Current Location

Most of the treasures excavated at Ur are in the [[British Museum]], the [[University of Pennsylvania Museum of Archaeology and Anthropology]], and the [[Baghdad Museum]]. In 2011, the Penn Museum opened the exhibition "Iraq's Ancient Past", featuring many pieces from the Royal Tombs.
